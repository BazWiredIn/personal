GenPf

Designed Regime Analysis: an evaluation pipeline for high-dimensional macroeconomic time series, combining non-linear embeddings (t-SNE), clustering, and nearest-neighbor logic. The system maps user-specified economic conditions to historically similar regimes while explicitly eliminating thin or unstable clusters, with systematic sensitivity analysis over embedding and clustering hyperparameters to study robustness and interpretability. About me:

I’ve always loved math, both for its structure and for what it makes possible.

In high school, that curiosity pushed me to self-study calculus and challenge the AP Calculus BC exam as a pre-calculus student, so I could take multivariable calculus as a senior and understand physics more deeply. I interned at Boeing working on radiation mitigation, set on studying mechanical engineering or physics after high school.

Then I came to Berkeley and took CS61A. I wrote my first line of code, and something clicked. Computation gave math leverage.

Since then, I’ve followed that thread through optimization, machine learning, and systems — working on multi-expert routing at Amazon, economic regime detection at GenPf, real-time vision systems at AlectoAI, and most recently AI-driven civic software at CityStreams.

I don’t plan my path very deliberately. I follow whatever pulls at my curiosity — usually problems where math meets the real world through code.
